Introduction

Waterloo is a famous battle fought in the year 1815. It
was between France, Prussia and England. It was the
last battle in the Napoleonic war. In the war there were
times in which either the British and Prussians or the
French could have one. But due to a few blundering
errors and a good deal of luck the Coalition managed to
win a decisive victory against Napoleon. 

Causes

  1. Napoleon's escape from Elba. Napoleon Bonaparte the great emperor and conqueror of Europe had been defeated in the hands of the sixth coalition. In the terms of the surrender he was to abdicate (give up his throne) and go on exile to Elba. Elba is an island near enough to Italy. It was a far cry from Napoleon's palace in France. But having sensed that the Bourbons (The Bourbons where the new family of the monarch) were very disliked by the French people (since of course all French people hate Monarchies and anything to do with it and they had fought a revolution to get it out of the way). He conspired to go back to France and reclaim his throne.
  2. The state of the Coalition's alliance. Prior to the forming of the coalition that had beaten Napoleon in the Battle of Leipzig and at first looked like there would be long term peace, there had been a lot of countries switching sides. Whether they were forced to like the Prussians or to some degree on there own incentive such as Russia it was almost absurd and really hard to remember whether a country in one particular time was with Napoleon or against him. The fact and point I am trying to make is that the the sixth coalition was not formed since Britain and Russia liked each other but because they both hated Napoleon. In the post war talks the Coalition was to preoccupied with debating over Napoleon's exile to Elba than actually taking an eye on him. This led to Napoleon having an almost frighteningly easy return trip to France. 
  3. The third and final cause of the Hundred Days Campaign and thus the battle of Waterloo is that France was in a way sore on their defeat to the coalition. They had been invaded some accounts say that they many villages had been pillaged but most historians diagram. But most of all especially for the Grande Armee of France is that a Monarchy in the Bourbons had been restored. All that they had fought for was ruined. Even if Napoleon did self proclaim him to be the emperor of France he was nonetheless a very capable general and he had created an empire. Support for Napoleon was far more than the support for their monarchy.

The Road to Waterloo

Now that we have discussed the causes of the Hundred Days Campaign, we must discuss the two battles immediately prior to Waterloo.

  1. Ligny (16 May 1815) -  In this battle Napoleon commanded the French army against the Prussians. Though the Prussians fought valiantly they were eventually pushed out.
  2. Quatre-Bras (16 May 1815) - On the same day Arthur Wellesley, the Duke of Wellington, commanded the British army at Quatre Bras against Michel Ney of the French. Even though the coalition was able to keep hold to a field which held a key position, the French had achieved their main aim which was to prevent the British and Prussians from coming to each other's aid...

The Battle of Waterloo

Now we will discuss the events which occurred in
Waterloo in which the Prussians and British end
Napoleon's hope to become the ruler of France (At this
point he was not hoping for an empire but rather to have
peace talks in a position where he can actually bargain
for something).

The Opposing Armies

The Imperial French Army was led by Emperor Napoleon Bonaparte. He was assisted by his marshals Michel Ney, Emmanuel de Grouchy and Jean de dieu Soult. During the Waterloo campaign, Napoleon commanded a strength of 126,000 men and 247 cannons.

Opposing the French was the British Army and led by the Duke of Wellington and the Prussian Army led by Marshal Gebhard von Bl√ºcher. These armies had a strength of 107,000 men (with 157 cannons) and 123,000 men (with 250 cannons) respectively. 

Battle Plans

As you can see in the armies total strength above the Prussians and British had more men combined against Napoleon but they had less when they were on their own. Thus, Napoleon's plan was to try and beat the British in Waterloo and to then beat the Prussians. The problem is that this depended on the Prussians and British army to not link up. To stop this from happening Napoleon sent Marshal Grouchy with a small force to harass the Prussians.

Wellington's Plan was the opposite. He aimed to hold off the French until the Prussians arrived. To do this he fortified three main positions in front of his army. These positions were La haye Sainte, Hougoumont and Papelotte. Each of these three positions were a problem to Napoleon. Hougoumont and Papelotte secured Wellington's flanks (If an opponent takes you flanks you run the risk of being surrounded) and La haye Sainte was right in the center of the Battlefield. Also Wellington was able to conceal most of his army behind a hill so he can sprite the French later.

Timeline of the Battle

The stage is set for the battle at Waterloo which occurred on 18 June 1815. 

9.00 am

Napoleon delays the attack

In the morning Napoleon wanted to quickly attack the
British but unfortunately the ground was to muddy for his cavalry(horsemen) and artillery. This meant that
Napoleon had to wait until the ground had frozen to
make his attack. This would prove to be costly in the
end. 

11.30 am

Napoleon attacks Hougoumont

The first major confrontation between the French and
British would be when Napoleon sent a few brigades to
attack the fortified Hougoumont. Since this was at the
flank of Wellington's army Napoleon knew this was a
key point and if he can just take it, victory would be his.
The French charge at Hougoumont almost worked. In
fact the French almost got past the front gate and that
would have been it for the British. But the British troops courageously fought off the French out of the gates and they were able to close the gate. Later Wellington stated that "the success of the battle turned upon closing the gates at Hougoumont".

11.30 am

 The Grand artillery bombardment

Realising Hougoumont would not fall so easily,
Napoleon decided to bombard the centre La Haye
Sainte. The problem was that the artillery were too far
back to accurately hit the British troops. Surprisingly the French were able to hit their mark and force the British slightly back.

12.00 am

The Prussians arrive

Though up to this point the British were doing quite well, Wellington had thrown a bit too much troops to defend Hougoumont. He famously said "Either nightfall or the Prussians must come if we are to win". When both he and Napoleon saw an army of men neither of the men knew who they were as they were too far to tell. Was it Marshall Grouchy or General Blucher? Both knew that this would decide the battle's outcome and perhaps the war itself. Then news came. The Prussians had arrived. Wellington was delighted. Napoleon on the other hand said to Ney to tell his men that Grouchy had arrived. This was to prevent the French morale from dropping. As Napoleon once said "in war, morale is everything". 

1.30 pm

French Infantry's first attack

After the Prussians had arrived, the first French infantry division. Napoleon still believed that since the Prussians had not immediately joined the battle and there was a long way to go for the Prussians that he could win the battle. He sent some of his reserves to face Blucher's army. He then ordered the infantry to march in columns (rows and rows of men quite tightly packed together). Most of them were supposed to attack La haye Sainte and the few others to relieve the men fighting to capture Hougoumont.

2.00 pm

The British Cavalry charge

The British army then sent their cavalry to go face the
French. While individually the British would win a one on one fight with the French cavalry, they did not attack as a group really well. Even Wellington said "Our officers of cavalry have acquired a trick of galloping at everything. They never consider the situation, never think of manoeuvring before an enemy, and never keep back or provide a reserve." The Scots are said to have made the bulk of the cavalry charge and they were able to a certain degree break the French line. The problem began when the British Cavalry advanced too far into the French army...

2.30 pm

The French Cavalry Counterattack

Realising the British Cavalry had gone to far into the
French line, the French cavalry set out to attack and
capture the cavalry of the British. Just like most of the
events that took place in Waterloo this only partially
worked. The French did take out a good many of the
British Cavalry but there were still a lot of British men
who were able to retreat to their line. 

4.00 pm

The French cavalry attack on the British line

Close to 4 pm Ney seemed to have notice the British to retreat. Seeing this Ney decided to try and go out for personal glory. He gathered a large cavalry force to try and take out the British. When Ney did this he committed two mortal sins.
1. The British were not retreating but were hiding
behind a hill.
2. The more important sin he committed was when he
only used his cavalry in the charge.
When the French cavalry arrived the British formed into
anti-cavalry squares used to stop cavalry units right in their tracks. Squares consisted of many men forming an outer square with their bayonets out while people in the middle would fire at the frightened horses. This
movement demolished the French cavalry and Ney was
forced to retreat. This in my opinion was one of the main reasons the French lost.
Eventually the French after many attempts were able to capture La haye Sainte at 6.30 pm but it was too late. At 4.00 pm the Prussians had started to attack Placenoit (a town at the French rear). This forced Napoleon to commit more men to face the French. 

7.30 pm

The French old guard launches a desparate attack

Realising that almost all hope was lost Napoleon had
one last trick up his sleeve. He was going to release the
French old guard into the mix. These were veterans of
war; they were the finest soldiers of Europe. But even
they could not could not break the British line. It was too late for them. There is speculation on what would have happened if the French old guard had been put into battle earlier. When the French old guard could not break the British line news spread around the French army saying "La Garde recule. Sauve qui peut!" ("The Guard is retreating. Every man for himself!"). At that time Placenoit was taken by the Prussians. 
It was all over for Napoleon; he had lost Waterloo. 

Did you know?
The British army especially Wellington respected the French guard (pictured) and feared them. Wellington gave them a choice to surrender. But since they were the French old guard the French reply was"La Garde
meurt, elle ne se rend pas!" ("The Guard dies, it does not surrender!"). They were then destroyed by artillery fire.

Aftermath

The main points of what happened after the battle was:

  • The Napoleonic wars was over.
  • Napoleon had to abdicate his throne again.
  • Michel Ney was executed for his treason (He was sent to capture Napoleon when Napoleon ecsaped Elba bu ended up joining him).
  • Napoleon was sent into exile to Saint Helene.
  • Peace would be in the continent until the first world war.
